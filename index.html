Core Definition & Practice

Academic Literacy (AL) is not just about reading and writing; itâ€™s about mastering the **social and discourse practices** specific to higher education.

It encompasses comprehension, composition, and the critical engagement with academic knowledge structures. Fundamentally, it's about navigating the "rules of the game" in university contexts, often implicitly.

AL is the ability to engage with, interpret, and produce texts within the constraints and conventions of disciplinary communities.

The Three Models (AL, AS, NE)

Barton and Hamilton's frameworks distinguish between three main models:

  1. Academic Literacy (AL): Focuses on disciplinary conventions and academic language.
  2. Academic Skills (AS): Treats literacy as general, transferable skills (e.g., summarizing, note-taking).
  3. New Literacies (NE): Acknowledges literacy as socially situated practices (the most dominant modern view).
The shift from the 'skills' model to the 'social practices' model revolutionized educational theory and practice.

Understanding these models helps educators tailor their support to move beyond basic skill deficits toward deeper **sociocultural engagement**.

Multimodality & Digital Literacy

In the digital age, academic literacy must extend to **multimodality**â€”the use of multiple communication modes (visual, audio, spatial, gestural, linguistic) to create meaning.

A multimodal academic task might involve creating a video essay, designing an infographic research summary, or producing a digital narrative.

"Meaning is made by the interplay of different modes, not just language." - The New London Group.

Digital literacy includes the ability to critically evaluate online sources, understand digital ethics, and navigate information ecosystems effectively. This is crucial for **21st-century academic success**.

Decolonial Insights & Ubuntu

A decolonial perspective challenges the **Eurocentric assumptions** embedded in traditional academic literacy frameworks. It asks: Whose knowledge and whose language are prioritized?

The **Ubuntu philosophy** provides a powerful alternative, centering on "I am because we are". Applied to literacy, it emphasizes collaborative knowledge-making, ethical relations, and community-based scholarship.

This approach moves beyond individual mastery to focus on literacy as a communal tool for **social justice and epistemic diversity**.

Decolonial AL necessitates a rejection of the 'deficit' view of students' home literacies.
